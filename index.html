02 Scope & Description

Navigating a large academic conference like ICML can be a daunting experience, particularly for those early in their careers or new to the community. The unstructured nature of coffee breaks and receptions often leads to what can only be described as "academic flocking," where attendees gravitate toward familiar faces, inadvertently creating invisible barriers to new connections.

This social, "How to Network at an AI Conference," aims to demystify the art of professional connection by transforming it from an anxiety-inducing ordeal into an engaging, fruitful activity — a structured yet informal session equipping attendees with practical, evidence-based strategies for building meaningful professional relationships.

Our goal is to move beyond the superficial exchange of pleasantries and business cards. Drawing on research into social dynamics and network theory, we'll explore how genuine, memorable interactions actually form.

Successful networking is less about the quantity of connections and more about the quality of interaction — shared interests and authentic curiosity, not collected contacts. Casciaro, Gino & Kouchaki, 2016

Research also suggests that adopting a growth mindset toward social skills meaningfully improves outcomes in professional settings. This social provides a low-stakes environment for like-minded participants to practice these principles, breaking the complex dance of conference socializing into a series of manageable — even enjoyable — steps.

The tone will be lighthearted and slightly amusing, acknowledging the inherent awkwardness of networking while giving actionable advice to overcome it. The organizing team is itself a living embodiment of the message: seven people from six institutions across academia and industry, who connected through the very kind of communication this session teaches.

04 Planned Activities

0:00–0:15

The "Why" and "How" of Conference Networking

Organizers deliver a concise, engaging presentation, humorously addressing networking pitfalls and offering a simple framework for conversation to set a collaborative tone.

0:15–1:00

Structured Icebreakers & Academic Speed-Networking

Attendees engage in brief, guided conversations in pairs or small groups, using prompts designed to spark substantive discussion:

  • "Discuss a paper you've read recently that changed your perspective."
  • "What's the most unexpected challenge you've faced in your research?"
1:00–1:45

Round-Table Discussions: Thematic Deep Dives

Attendees gather at round tables, each carrying a designated theme, facilitated by an organizer or volunteer so everyone gets a chance to contribute:

  • Navigating academia vs. industry
  • Finding collaborators for side projects
  • Tips for engaging with senior researchers
1:45–2:00

Wrap-Up & Open Mingling

Participants mingle freely — reconnecting with people met earlier or starting new conversations with their newly practiced skills, unstructured and self-directed.

The format is deliberately interactive throughout, keeping the focus on social connection and community-building in the spirit of ICML Socials.
